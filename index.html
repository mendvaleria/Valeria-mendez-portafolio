Chronically online White House

Chronically online White house places viewers inside the X feed of the White House, revealing how political messaging is increasingly filtered through memes, aestheticized violence, and algorithmic trends. Drawing on real posts from the Trump administration’s official X account, the project exposes how digital platforms are used to normalize nationalist imagery and fascist undertones. As users scroll, they’re immersed in a curated stream where entertainment and propaganda merge.

This project was part of „Digital Bodies“ one night show at @frappant_kachelraum 16.7.2025.

★ ★ ★ MARICA BOXER SHORTS ★★★

2025

“Marica” is a Spanish slang term with multiple meanings that vary across contexts and cultures. Historically, it has been used as a derogatory word to insult someone by questioning their masculinity or implying weakness—often directed toward LGBTQIA+ individuals. Its origin and use are deeply tied to machismo, a cultural norm in many Spanish-speaking societies that reinforces traditional gender roles and ideals of masculinity.

In recent years, however, the meaning of marica has evolved. Some members of the LGBTQ+ community have reclaimed the term, transforming it into a symbol of pride, resistance, and solidarity. In several parts of Latin America—especially in Colombia—it is also used casually and even affectionately among friends. Nevertheless, it remains a word that can still carry offense, depending on context, tone, and intent.

This project was a collaboration with illustrator Jenny Vieler, combining design and illustration to explore the cultural and emotional layers of language, identity, and reclaiming expression.

nos vivimos muriendo / wir sterben, also wir leben

Nos Vivimos Muriendo is the graduation project of Colombian director Iván Ruge, a musical theatre performance that examines death from diverse perspectives within Latin American and Caribbean realities. The production intertwines fiction and reality, using musical genres such as salsa, cumbia, reggaetón, and mambo to explore the cultural, emotional, and social layers surrounding death.

The piece reflects on mortality as a space for dialogue about social inequality, tradition, and collective memory, portraying how these dimensions shape our relationship with loss. For this project, I developed the visual identity, including the poster, flyers, program booklet, and social media design for @nosvivimosmuriendo, creating a cohesive visual language that connected the themes of life, death, and celebration.

2024 Coca is not Cocaine

For over 4,000 years, the coca plant has been central to Andean life—sacred, healing, and deeply woven into indigenous culture. Once a symbol of spirituality and unity, coca’s meaning has become polarized. While still revered by indigenous peoples, its image is now overshadowed by its links to cocaine, violence, and global trade. The collages explore coca’s transformation from sacred leaf to symbol of conflict, focusing on Colombia’s history of cultivation, commodification, and corruption. Through a series of digital collages made from archival materials and screen printed, it reveals how cultural heritage, politics, and violence intersect.
